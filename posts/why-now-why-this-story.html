Why now, why this one

Bundy gets four miniseries. Dahmer gets a Ryan Murphy. Gaskins — who, by his own count, killed more people than both of them combined — has waited fifty years for a camera.

By Marlowe Hatcher

There are a lot of true crime stories that don't get told on film. Not because they're uninteresting — in fact, the ones that don't get made often have more to say than the ones that do. They don't get told because the machinery of film production has to believe there's an audience, and the machinery has been very slow to look south and slow to look away from the usual suspects.

Ted Bundy has four prestige documentaries. Dahmer has Netflix. But Donald "Pee Wee" Gaskins — who admitted to killing more than 110 people, who was active in the same era as Bundy, who operated across the exact geography that should have made him famous — has spent fifty years in relative obscurity outside the Carolinas.

Part of that is geography. The Pee Dee region isn't Los Angeles or New York or even the popular murder-destination cities that documentaries love. Part of it is access. Bundy left behind a charismatic mythology that Hollywood is always happy to amplify. Gaskins left behind crime scenes and people who just want to forget.

But I think the main reason is this: the industry has only recently started to believe that stories about rural crime, told from the perspective of rural people, are worth telling at all.

What changed

A few things happened at the same time. True crime as a genre exploded after about 2015, and it pulled in a lot of new storytellers who had their own relationships to these stories. Women filmmakers started making documentaries. Regional filmmakers started getting funding. And the audience that was interested in crime stories started asking for more complexity — not just what happened, but why, and to whom, and what did we miss.

That created space for a story like Gaskins's to finally surface. Not because it's suddenly more interesting — it's been interesting all along — but because someone finally decided it was worth the investment.

The actual case, in outline

I'll do a full primer in a separate post, but here's the headline version: Donald Gaskins grew up in South Carolina in the 1950s, had a childhood that included violence and sexual assault, and spent most of his adult life either incarcerated or in the communities between Florence and Myrtle Beach. He killed people — by his own admission, more than 110. He was convicted of a fraction of that number. He was executed in 1991.

The reason the case is interesting isn't the body count. It's the texture of it. Gaskins killed as part of a very specific social geography — hitchhikers, rideshare passengers, people he knew through work. He cultivated an image as safe. He was the friend who'd give you a ride. And for decades, no one put the pattern together.

Why this story, right now

There are a few things about the Gaskins case that feel particularly urgent right now. The first is accountability — Gaskins operated in plain sight, and the institutions around him (police, social services, the people who knew him) failed to see or act on what was happening. That's a story about system failure, and it's a story we're newly interested in telling.

The second is geography and class. The Gaskins story is a story about a region that doesn't usually get to tell its own stories. It's not a story where the killer is a genius or charismatic — it's a story where the killer is one bad person in a complex place, and the complexity of the place matters.

The third is this: a lot of the women Gaskins killed are still unnamed, still unclaimed, still not integrated into the true crime narrative. Their absence is the story. Blacktop's film is finally going to have to reckon with that.
