Why now, why this one

Bundy gets four miniseries. Dahmer gets a Ryan Murphy. Gaskins — who, by his own count, killed more people than both of them combined — has waited fifty years for a camera. I have theories.

By Marlowe Hatcher

I keep a list, in a Field Notes book that's falling apart, of the cases I think will never be filmed. It's not a serious list. It's a thing I do at the library when I'm waiting for someone to bring up a microfilm spool. But I've kept it long enough that I've started noticing which entries get crossed off.

Gaskins has been on that list since 2019. I crossed him off last week.

The waiting

Bundy's first dramatization came out in 1986, five years after his trial. Dahmer's first feature came in 2002, eight years after his death. John Wayne Gacy got To Catch a Killer in 1992, six years after his trial finished. The general American serial-killer-biopic schedule, if there is one, looks like this: high-profile case, prestige book or two, then a TV movie within a decade, then in subsequent decades a steady drip of theatrical features, miniseries, Ryan Murphys.

Gaskins's case finished, in any meaningful sense, in 1991, when South Carolina executed him. We are thirty-five years past the end. There has not, in those thirty-five years, been a single English-language feature film about him. A handful of documentary segments — one solid hour on a basic-cable countdown show, the rest cursory — and Wilton Earle's biography Final Truth, which has stayed in and out of print, and that is essentially it.

So: why the wait, and why is the wait ending now?

Theory one: the geography

Most of the American killers who get the prestige treatment were operating in places with strong media gravity. Bundy moved through the Pacific Northwest and Florida; Dahmer's case broke in Milwaukee but was litigated nationally because of the racial politics of who he killed and how the police had failed them; Gacy was a Chicago story. These cases came pre-translated for the coastal industry, with court records in major-paper archives and surviving witnesses in cities reporters could find on a Monday morning.

Gaskins's case happened in Florence County, South Carolina, in towns most people in his own state would have trouble locating on a map. The contemporaneous reporting is scattered across small papers, much of it still un-digitized. To make a film about him in 1995 you would have had to send a researcher to Prospect for a month. Not impossible — true-crime publishers have done much worse — but enough friction to push the project down the slate.

Theory two: he confessed too much

This sounds backwards, and it is, a little, but stay with me. The Bundy myth is built on his refusal to confess; the Dahmer myth is built on the clinical horror of what he confessed to. Gaskins's confessions — first to Earle, eventually to anyone with a tape recorder — were so voluminous, so contradictory, and so deliberately self-mythologizing that they make a clean script almost impossible to write. He claimed over a hundred victims. Investigators confirmed somewhere between nine and fourteen, depending on whom you ask. The number that was true was somewhere between those, and the truth-shaped middle is exactly the kind of ambiguity that biopic financiers have historically refused to fund.

What's changed, I think, is the genre's appetite for ambiguity. Mindhunter, I'll Be Gone in the Dark, Wormwood — the last decade taught a generation of audiences to sit with not-knowing. A film that opens with we will never have a final number is, in 2026, an artistic stance. In 1996 it was a financing problem.

Theory three: the victims weren't the right victims

I'm going to write this carefully, because the dignity of the people in question matters to me. The American serial-killer story, as commercially packaged, has historically required victims the white middle-class viewer would recognize as themselves or as their daughters. The Gaskins victims, almost all of them, were rural Southern young people — many of them Black, many of them poor, many of them people the local press buried inside their papers or did not name at all. The press treated their deaths as a smaller event than the case warranted, and a film industry that takes its cues from that press did the same.

The story of the last ten years in true crime, both critically and commercially, has been the slow correction of that. Audiences have demonstrated, repeatedly, that they will sit through a film that takes overlooked victims seriously. I don't know whether Gaskins: The Hitchhiker Killer will do that. The poster's restraint is a hopeful sign; the title isn't. I'll be watching.

Theory four: it was always going to take an indie

This is the simplest one, and probably the truest. A major studio was never going to make this film, because a major studio is not going to greenlight a $20m biopic about a regional killer most of the audience has never heard of. Blacktop is a small distributor with a habit of taking the kinds of swings major studios won't. The film exists because someone there cared enough to push it through, and because the budget is small enough that it didn't need a Bundy-shaped opening weekend.

That's the most encouraging thing I can say about the project, and it's the reason I'm trying to keep my expectations reasonable. The Gaskins case has waited a long time for a serious treatment. A small indie willing to do it carefully is, I think, the best version of the camera finally arriving.
