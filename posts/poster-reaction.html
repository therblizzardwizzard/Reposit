Sitting with the Gaskins one-sheet

Blacktop dropped the poster last week. I've had it open in a tab for four days. A few notes, mostly about the eyes.

By Marlowe Hatcher

One-sheet courtesy of Blacktop. Used here for commentary and review.

I don't think a poster has made me put my coffee down in a while, but this one did. I want to be careful here — I haven't seen a frame of the film, and a poster is just a poster, a marketing object designed to do exactly what this one is doing to me. Still: I've been staring at it, and the staring is the post.

The first thing you notice is the eyes. Whoever cropped this image cropped it for the eyes. They're lit from above and slightly to the right, so one is in clean light and the other is half-lost in the brow's shadow — a split that does a lot of work without asking the actor to do anything. That's a very old portrait trick (Caravaggio had it down four hundred years ago) but it lands here because the face under it is so young.

The casting choice the poster makes

This is what I keep circling back to. Donald Gaskins was thirty-six when his confirmed murder spree began and forty-two when he was arrested for it. The man in this image is not playing a forty-two-year-old. He's playing a twenty-something, maybe a high-school-aged twenty-something, lean and unfinished. The poster is making a choice — a thesis — and the thesis is: this is who he was before anyone could see it.

I'm not sure how I feel about that thesis. There's an argument for it: most of what we know about Gaskins's early years comes from his own self-mythologizing in the Wilton Earle biography, where he describes being a violent and largely undetected child. A film that starts there is at least trying to ask how, which is the question the genre most often refuses. But the image is also doing the other thing a serial-killer movie poster always does, which is making a face into a question mark. "Look at him. You'd never have known." It's the same composition Monster used, and My Friend Dahmer, and three different Bundy projects. It works because it works.

The tagline

They all thought he'd let them live.

This is the line that tells me the script has, at minimum, read the case. Gaskins's documented method — and his own ugly testimony about it — turned on a deliberate cultivation of the moment of false reprieve. He took a long time with his victims, in part because the patience itself was the point. The tagline doesn't sensationalize that; it states it, flatly, in the voice of the people no longer here to correct it. It's the kind of line that earns the dignity I'm always asking these projects for.

It also, mercifully, does not include the words "hitchhiker killer" — which the title can't avoid, but which has always been an awkward fit. He killed hitchhikers and rideshare passengers (the seventies version) and people he was hired to kill and people who knew him well enough to get in the car. "Hitchhiker Killer" is what the AP called him in 1976, and the marketing department clearly decided it had brand recognition. Fair enough; that's what marketing departments do.

What Blacktop usually makes

One sentence on this, because I had to look it up: Blacktop is a small, mostly genre distributor — the kind of shop that puts out two or three films a year, often regional, often with a true-story hook. Their track record is uneven but not exploitative, which is more than I'd say for some bigger players in this space. The poster's restraint reads consistent with their better releases. I'm cautiously interested.

What I'll be watching for

Three things, and I'll cover each in its own post once we have a trailer or a release window:

  1. How they handle the women. Gaskins's "Coastal Kills" were almost all women and girls. The biopic genre's instinct here is to put them onscreen as bodies; a serious film puts them onscreen as people first.
  2. How they handle the timeline. The case is structurally messy — multiple confessions, recanted confessions, "Serious Murders" versus "Coastal Kills," the prison killing that finally got the death sentence. There's a real risk of clean-narrative compression that lies about what was known when.
  3. How they handle Prospect. Specifically, the place. Gaskins's South Carolina is a real geography of a real period, and most of the projects I love in this genre let the location do half the moral work.

For now, though, it's just the poster. Yellow on dark, one eye lit, one eye not. They all thought he'd let them live. I'm going to go reread chapter three of Earle and try not to think about it for the rest of the afternoon.

Next up: a piece I've been drafting for a month about why this story finally got made — and what took the rest of the industry so long.
