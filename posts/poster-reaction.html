Sitting with the Gaskins one-sheet

Blacktop dropped the poster last week. I've had it open in a tab for four days. A few notes, mostly about the eyes.

By Marlowe Hatcher

One-sheet courtesy of Blacktop. Used here for commentary and review.

I don't think a poster has made me put my coffee down in a while, but this one did. I want to be careful here — I haven't seen a frame of the film, and a poster is just a poster, a marketing artifact. But this one has something happening in it.

The first thing you notice is the eyes. Whoever cropped this image cropped it for the eyes. They're lit from above and slightly to the right, so one is in clean light and the other is half in shadow. The face itself is mostly shadow. The eyes are everything.

The casting choice the poster makes

This is what I keep circling back to. Donald Gaskins was thirty-six when his confirmed murder spree began and forty-two when he was arrested for it. The man in this image is not playing a killer in the moment. He's playing the idea of a killer before he knew it. He's playing a version of Gaskins when he was still someone else.

I'm not sure how I feel about that thesis. There's an argument for it: most of what we know about Gaskins's early years comes from his own self-mythologizing in the Wilton Earle biography — the childhood violence, the attempted rapes, the "practical jokes" that escalated. If Blacktop is interested in the question of how someone becomes someone, then starting with a young face makes sense.

The tagline

They all thought he'd let them live.

This is the line that tells me the script has, at minimum, read the case. Gaskins's documented method — and his own ugly testimony about it — turned on a deliberate cultivation of the assumption that he wasn't a threat. He was the chauffeur, the friend, the ride home. He was safe.

It also, mercifully, does not include the words "hitchhiker killer" — which the title can't avoid, but which has always been an awkward fit. He killed hitchhikers and rideshare passengers, people in his social circle, people he had access to as someone's boyfriend or employee.

What Blacktop usually makes

One sentence on this, because I had to look it up: Blacktop is a small, mostly genre distributor — the kind of shop that puts out two or three films a year, often regional, often with a point of view. They're not making prestige documentaries; they're making films with an argument.

What I'll be watching for

Three things, and I'll cover each in its own post once we have a trailer or a release window:

  1. How they handle the women. Gaskins's "Coastal Kills" were almost all women and girls. The biopic genre's instinct here is to put them onscreen as bodies; a serious film would ask why.
  2. How they handle the timeline. The case is structurally messy — multiple confessions, recanted confessions, "Serious Murders" versus "Coastal Kills," the prison killing. A lesser film linearizes it; a better one lets the mess do the work.
  3. How they handle Prospect. Specifically, the place. Gaskins's South Carolina is a real geography of a real period, and most of the projects I love in this genre let the landscape be a character.

For now, though, it's just the poster. Yellow on dark, one eye lit, one eye not. They all thought he'd let them live. I'm going to go reread chapter three of Earle and try not to think about how a poster design can do so much work in a few inches.

Next up: a piece I've been drafting for a month about why this story finally got made — and what took the rest of the industry so long.
