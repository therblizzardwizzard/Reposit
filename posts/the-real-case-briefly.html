The real case, briefly

For readers landing here from the trailer chatter: a short, sourced walk through who Donald "Pee Wee" Gaskins actually was, what he actually did, and how he ended.

By Marlowe Hatcher

Donald Henry Gaskins Jr. was born in 1933 in South Carolina and spent most of his life in and around the Pee Dee region — Florence, Mullins, Prospect, that stretch of mostly rural country between the piedmont and the coast.

His childhood included violence. His mother was violent. The homes he was placed in as a foster child were violent. By his own account (which we should take with caution), he was sexually abused and raped multiple times. There's a pattern here that will feel familiar if you've read any criminal psychology — the feedback loop between victimization and offending.

The crimes

Gaskins's first confirmed killing was in 1967, when he was thirty-six years old. He killed a man named Walter Neely who had picked him up hitchhiking. Over the next fifteen years, he killed again and again — some of the murders he classified as "Serious Murders" (killing for reason), and some as "Coastal Kills" (killing for satisfaction). The actual number is disputed. He confessed to 110. Prosecutors convicted him of 11. He may have killed more than either number.

What made his case unusual wasn't the body count — it was how invisible he remained. Gaskins wasn't a celebrity killer. He was a chauffeur, a boyfriend, a friend's ex-roommate. He was someone you might hire for a ride, or meet through a friend of a friend. He killed hitchhikers and the people he met through work. He was, for years, just a guy.

The women he killed were often people without a lot of institutional attention — people whose disappearances weren't immediately investigated, whose bodies were never found, whose families had to spend years not knowing. That's part of what the case reveals about how we look for murder victims: we look for the visible ones first.

The arrest and trial

Gaskins was arrested in 1975 for a different crime — hitchhiking fraud. But the investigation led to murder charges. Over the next few years, he was indicted for multiple murders. His confessions were contradictory, sometimes graphic, sometimes suggestive that he was exaggerating for psychological effect. The legal system eventually convicted him of eleven murders, though he's believed to have killed many more.

The trial records and confessions are haunting. Gaskins was articulate about what he'd done, but often seemed more interested in the telling than in the actual crimes. Some forensic psychologists have read him as someone who was performing his own criminality for an audience. Others read him as someone who was simply telling the truth.

Death row and execution

Gaskins was sentenced to death in 1976. He spent fifteen years on death row, during which time he killed at least one other inmate. He was executed in 1991 by electric chair at Broad River Correctional Institution in Columbia, South Carolina.

In his final statement, he did not show remorse. He did not apologize. He spent it on a criticism of prison and society, and on a final expression of his own grievance.

Why this case matters

The Gaskins case is important because it reveals the gaps in how we investigate and prosecute murder. He operated in rural South Carolina for years — not as a hidden criminal, but as part of the visible social fabric — before anyone put the murders together. The victims he killed were disproportionately people without a lot of institutional visibility: women, the poor, hitchhikers. Their deaths were investigated slowly, if at all.

It's also a case about the American South in the 1970s — about poverty, about the institutions that fail people, about the social geography that allows predators to move through communities undisturbed.

And it's a case about how we tell crime stories. Gaskins has been almost entirely absent from the true crime media landscape. He's been, until recently, invisible in the culture. The question worth asking is: why?
