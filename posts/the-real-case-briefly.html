The real case, briefly

For readers landing here from the trailer chatter: a short, sourced walk through who Donald "Pee Wee" Gaskins actually was, what he actually did, and how he ended.

By Marlowe Hatcher

Content note. This post discusses real murders, including sexual violence and the deaths of minors. I've kept the descriptions as plain and as brief as I can while staying accurate. If you'd rather skip this one, the other posts on the site don't go into specifics.

Most of what's reliable about Gaskins comes from three places: court records from his 1976 trial in Florence County and his 1982 trial for the murder of Rudolph Tyner; contemporaneous reporting in The State, The News and Courier, and the AP wire; and the long, contested autobiography Final Truth, dictated to Wilton Earle in 1991 while Gaskins was on death row. The first two are conservative and well-documented. The third is, by Earle's own account, partly fabricated — Gaskins inflated, invented, and revised. I'll flag where the numbers come from when it matters.

Who he was

Donald Henry Gaskins was born in 1933 in Florence County, South Carolina, the unrecognized son of a single mother. He was small for his age and stayed small for the rest of his life — five-foot-five at his trial, the source of the nickname "Pee Wee," which the press picked up and which he hated. His childhood was institutional from the start: reform school by twelve, juvenile detention through his teens, the South Carolina state penitentiary in his early twenties for assault. He was out and in for the next decade and a half on a rotation of robbery, statutory rape, and parole violation.

He worked, intermittently, in roofing and carpentry. He was twice married. He had children. He had a long-running gray-market business buying and reselling stolen goods, which is part of how the murders began.

What he did

By the prosecution's case at his first capital trial, Gaskins committed nine murders between September 1973 and December 1975 in or near Florence and Sumter counties. His own later count, in Final Truth, was more than a hundred. Investigators, working from physical evidence and confessions corroborated by remains, eventually credited him with somewhere between twelve and fourteen. The exact number is one of the things this case will never fully settle.

What's clear about the method, and what the contemporaneous record supports, is this. He sorted his killings, in his own telling, into two categories: Coastal Kills, in which he picked up hitchhikers and rideshare passengers — overwhelmingly young women, several of them children — along U.S. 17 and the surrounding state highways, and killed them in remote locations; and Serious Murders, which he carried out on people he knew personally, often for money or in retaliation. Several of the Serious Murders were contract killings paid for by others, and at least one of those clients was eventually prosecuted alongside him.

The Coastal Kills are the most documented aspect of his cruelty. He took time with his victims, deliberately, and what time he took is not something I'm going to detail here. The court record from the 1976 trial is publicly available for anyone who needs to see it; the press accounts at the time, even by the standards of seventies tabloid reporting, were unusually restrained, because what they would otherwise have had to print was unprintable.

How he was caught

He wasn't, in the cinematic sense, hunted down. He was given up. In late 1975 a former associate named Walter Neely, who had been involved in several of the Serious Murders, broke under questioning by Florence County deputies on an unrelated charge and led them to a burial site on Gaskins's property in Prospect. Investigators recovered eight bodies. The case moved quickly from there.

Gaskins was convicted in May 1976 on nine counts of murder and sentenced to death. That sentence was commuted to life in 1977 when the U.S. Supreme Court's ruling in Woodson v. North Carolina invalidated South Carolina's mandatory death-penalty scheme.

The prison murder

The reason he was ultimately executed, rather than dying in prison of old age, was a single additional killing he committed while incarcerated. In 1982, on contract from the family of one of his victims, Gaskins murdered Rudolph Tyner — a fellow death-row inmate convicted of a 1978 double homicide — by smuggling a small explosive device into Tyner's cell concealed in a radio speaker. It was, by any measure, an extraordinary act of operational patience and the only one of his crimes that the press covered nationally in real time. He was sentenced to death for it in 1983 and exhausted his appeals by 1991.

South Carolina executed him on September 6, 1991, in the electric chair at Broad River Correctional Institution. He was the last person executed by electrocution in the state until the legislature reauthorized the method in 2021. He was fifty-eight.

What the film will have to choose

I'll get into this in more depth in a later post, but the short version: any feature treatment of the Gaskins case has to pick a number (the official, the inflated, or the genuinely unknown), a structure (chronological, frame-narrative through Earle, or post-conviction looking back), and a frame on the victims (named individuals, or a composite). Each of those choices is, in essence, an argument about what kind of story this was. I'm curious which arguments Blacktop's screenwriters have decided to make, and which they've decided to leave for the next film someone makes about him, in another fifty years or sooner.

Sources for this post: trial transcripts via the South Carolina state archives; AP wire reporting collected in the South Caroliniana Library; Wilton Earle, Final Truth: The Autobiography of a Serial Killer (1992), used cautiously and with attribution where any contested fact appears.
